The Monty Hall Problem

Suppose you're on a game show, and you're given the choice of three doors. Behind one door is a car and behind the other two are goats. You pick a door, say door number 1, and the host, who knows what's behind the doors, opens another door, say door number 3, and reveals the first goat. He then says to you, "Do you want to stay with the first door you picked, or would you like to switch and pick door number 2?".

Now most people (including myself once upon a time), would assume that since you're down to two doors, the chances of winning are 50/50 regardless of whether you decide to stick with your first choice or switch to another door. Believe it or not, this isn't true!

The History Behind This Problem

The Monty Hall problem is a brain teaser, in the form of a probability puzzle, loosely based on the American television game show Let's Make a Deal and named after its original host, Monty Hall. The problem was originally posed in a letter by Steve Selvin in 1990 to Marilyn vos Savant's "Ask Marilyn" column.

Vos Savant's response was that the contestant should switch to the other door. She argued that contestants who switch have a 2 in 3 chance of winning the car, while contestants who stick to their initial choice have only a 1 in 3 chance.

Not convinced? Well you aren't alone. Many readers of vos Savant's column refused to believe switching is a superior strategy. After the problem appeared in her column, approximately 10,000 readers, including nearly 1,000 with PhDs, wrote to the magazine, most of them claiming vos Savant was wrong. Paul Erd≈ës, one of the most prolific mathematicians in history, remained unconvinced until he was shown a computer simulation demonstrating vos Savant's predicted result.

Understanding the solution conceptually

A lot of people struggle understanding how on Earth the odds aren't always 50/50. The key here is that you are NOT making a completely 'blind' guess between the two remaining doors. The Monty Hall problem has a very specific clause: Monty, the host of the show, knows where the car is. He is the one who eliminates one of the doors that you did not select. When he eliminates the doors (i.e. shows you what's behind the door you did NOT select) he never chooses the door with the car. Because Monty will never eliminate the door that holds the car, he raises the odds that switching is always a good bet.

Now this can be difficult to wrap your head around with only 3 doors, so let's look at the exact same problem with 100 doors instead of 3. You pick a random door. Instead of one door, Monty now eliminates 98 doors.These are doors that he knows do not have the prize! This leaves only two doors. The one you picked, and one that was left after Monty eliminated the others. Do you still feel like sticking with the door you first picked when there were 100 options is just as likely to result in a win than the remaining door that Monty himself made sure stays in the game?
